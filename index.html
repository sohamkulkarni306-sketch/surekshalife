The basics

What life insurance actually does

Strip away the jargon and it's a simple contract: you pay a premium, and the insurer pays a lump sum the "sum assured" to the people you name. If you pass away during the policy term.

◆

It replaces your income

If your family depends on your salary, a payout gives them years of runway to adjust rent, school fees, loans without a financial cliff.

◆

It clears your debts

A home loan or personal loan doesn't pause because you're gone. Cover sized to your liabilities keeps them off your family's shoulders.

◆

It can also build wealth

Beyond pure protection, endowment, ULIP and retirement plans combine a payout with savings or market linked returns at a higher cost.

The process

How to buy your first policy

Six steps, in order. Do them in this sequence and you'll avoid the two mistakes beginners make most: being underinsured, and buying the wrong type of plan.

01

Work out how much cover you need

A common starting rule: 10–15× your annual income, plus outstanding loans, minus existing savings. Add school or wedding costs for your children if relevant.

02

Choose the type of plan

Term insurance gives pure, high value protection at a low premium the right default for most beginners. Endowment, ULIP and whole-life plans cost more and mix in savings.

03

Shortlist insurers, not just premiums

Get quotes from 3 to 4 companies. Then weigh the Claim Settlement Ratio and Solvency Ratio alongside the price — a cheaper premium means little if claims aren't paid smoothly. Use the Compare page for this.

04

Disclose everything, honestly

Income, smoking, health history, existing policies all of it. Non disclosure is the single biggest reason claims get rejected later, when it's too late to fix.

05

Buy online directly, or via a licensed broker

Complete eKYC, review the policy illustration (not just the brochure), and pay. Online term plans are typically cheaper than the same cover bought through an agent.

06

Nominate, store, and revisit

Name a clear nominee, keep the policy document and receipts somewhere your family can find them, and use the freelook period to double check everything before it locks in.

For first time buyers

Ten things beginners should get right

The plan you pick matters less than avoiding these common, expensive mistakes.

✓

Buy young

Premiums are locked to your age at purchase the earlier you buy, the cheaper it stays for the full term.

✓

Prioritise pure protection first

A term plan should usually come before investment linked insurance products.

✓

Don't under-insure

A small policy "to get started" often leaves a gap exactly when it's needed most.

✓

Read the exclusions

Waiting periods, suicide clauses, and specific exclusions vary — read the policy wording, not just the ad.

✓

Check claim settlement ratio

Look for insurers consistently above 97–98%, published annually by IRDAI.

✓

Consider useful riders

Critical illness and accidental death riders add meaningful cover for a modest extra premium.

✓

Compare, don't settle on one quote

Premiums for identical cover can vary meaningfully between insurers always get several quotes.

✓

Review every few years

A new loan, child, or salary jump usually means your cover needs to grow too.

✓

Know tax rules can change

Premiums often qualify for deductions under prevailing tax law verify current rules before assuming a benefit.

✓

Keep paperwork simple for your family

Share policy details with your nominee now, not as a surprise they have to search for later.

Common questions

Quick answers

Term insurance vs whole life — which should a beginner pick?

For most beginners, term insurance is the more efficient choice: it offers a much larger sum assured for a lower premium because it's pure protection with no savings component. Whole life and endowment plans cost more but return value even if you outlive the term — useful if you specifically want a savings element bundled in.

Is the cheapest premium the best choice?

Not on its own. A very low premium can reflect a leaner claims process or fewer built-in benefits. Weigh price against claim settlement ratio, solvency, and the insurer's reputation for service before deciding.

Can I buy life insurance entirely online?

Yes — most insurers and licensed brokers offer full digital journeys with e-KYC, instant quotes, and e-policy documents, generally regulated by IRDAI. Confirm you're on an authorised insurer or broker site before entering personal details.

What happens if I stop paying premiums?

Most policies have a grace period, after which the policy can lapse. Some plans allow revival within a set window. Check the specific policy's grace period and revival terms before you buy.

Claim Settlement Ratio

The share of claims an insurer paid out of all claims received in a year. Above 97% is generally considered strong and reliable.

Solvency Ratio

How much surplus capital an insurer holds against its liabilities. IRDAI mandates a minimum of 1.50 — higher means a stronger cushion during high claim volumes.
